The closest point to three seas in Italy

The investigation into the geography of the Italian peninsula presents numerous questions of great interest. Among these, one particularly fascinating inquiry involves the possibility of the existence of a point in Italy that is equidistant from three seas. This study aims to examine this question and, in doing so, determine whether such points exist, and if so, to locate them precisely.

The origin of the question

The initial question, formulated informally, originated from a query posed by Fabio, who suggested the idea that a point in Italy could be equidistant from three seas. This hypothesis led us to reflect on its uniqueness and the possibility that such a point could be located in Basilicata. However, the seemingly simple nature of the question required a more in-depth analysis, prompting the need to adopt a scientific approach to reach a definitive conclusion.

The research

The preliminary phase of the research was based on a qualitative observation of geographic maps, through which it was hypothesized that Basilicata was the most likely region to host a point equidistant from three seas. However, it was necessary to develop a more rigorous method to confirm this hypothesis. To this end, we considered implementing algorithms and mathematical models to accurately identify a potential equidistant point.

Methodology

The first challenge encountered was accessing sufficiently detailed data on coastal boundaries. After an initial phase of study on maps with approximate boundaries, we opted to use the borders provided by OpenStreetMap (OSM), which offer high resolution. This dataset, solely for the three Lucanian coasts, included a total of 18,423 points. The computational process was carried out using a program developed in TypeScript, utilizing Vue.js for graphical visualization of the results.

Three main methods were employed for determining the equidistant point:

  • Coastline projections:

    We traced perpendicular projections relative to the tangents of the coastal lines at each relevant point, gradually narrowing the area of interest. Although this methodology provided a preliminary indication of the area where the equidistant point might be located, the numerical approximations introduced by the discrete projection values limited the precision of the result.

  • Visual verification:

    After identifying candidate points, we proceeded with visual verification by drawing circles with a radius of ùëë, corresponding to the estimated distance from the coasts. This method qualitatively confirmed the equidistance of the selected points from the three coasts.

  • Refinement algorithm:

    The most rigorous method for determining the point was based on an iterative algorithm. The process involved selecting a range of coordinates and a progressively increasing level of detail. For each point within the range, the distance to the coasts was calculated using the haversine formula, and the point with the minimum deviation between the distances from the three seas was further refined, increasing precision until an error tolerance of less than 1e-9 was reached.

The result

The final result of our study led to the identification of a specific point, with coordinates 40.666241427559974, 16.03936215769008, located in Basilicata. This point is equidistant from the three seas considered, with a distance of 74.589 km from each coast. To further validate the result, reverse verification was performed, measuring the distances from the three coastal points to the identified point. The values obtained confirmed the equidistance, consolidating the correctness of our methodology and the reliability of the result.

Conclusions

Our research demonstrated the existence of a point equidistant from three seas in Italy, specifically in Basilicata, confirming the initial hypothesis. The method used allowed us to progressively narrow the search area, ultimately pinpointing the desired location.
